    Robust contracting under double moral hazard
    Abstract

    We study contracting when both principal and agent have to exert noncontractible effort for production to take place and the principal is uncertain about what actions are available to the agent. Any contract is evaluated by the guaranteed expected payoff for the principal no matter what actions the agent may take. Both parties are risk-neutral; there is no limited liability. Linear contracts, which leave the agent with a constant share of output in exchange for a fixed fee, are optimal. This result holds both in a preliminary version of the model where the principal only chooses to supply or not supply an input, and in several suitably-formulated variants of a more general version where the principal may have multiple choices of input. The model thus generates nontrivial linear sharing rules without relying on either limited liability or risk aversion.


    With Gabriel Carroll
  • The Role of Referrals in Inequality, Immobility, and Inefficiency in Labor Markets

    with Nicole Immorlica and Matt Jackson

    Abstract PDF SSRN Seminar Presentation
    The Role of Referrals in Inequality, Immobility, and Inefficiency in Labor Markets
    Abstract

    We study labor markets in which firms can hire either via referrals or open applications. Referrals screen candidates and lead to better matches and increased productivity, but disadvantage workers who apply via open applications. We identify conditions under which distributing referrals more evenly across a population not only reduces inequality, but also increases productivity and also improves economic mobility across generations. We use the model to examine optimal policies, showing that one-time affirmative action policies have long-term impacts due to induced changes in future referrals. We also show how the possibility of firing workers improves hiring and lowers inequality.


    With Nicole Immorlica and Matt Jackson.

    Motivated Mislearning
    Abstract

    Can individuals mislearn from mere repetitions of information when they are motivated to do so? In an ego-relevant setting, we find that participants mislearn more from repeated ego-favorable information than from repeated ego-unfavorable information. Effects are not explained by participants' inability to identify the redundant nature of such information. Consistent with the motivated reasoning mechanism, the differential effect is completely driven by participants who report a high importance of ego-favorable beliefs.


    With Tony Q. Fan
